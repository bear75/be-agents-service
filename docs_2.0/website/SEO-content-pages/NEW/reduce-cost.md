Reducing Costs in Home Care: 5 Ways Better Scheduling Can Help
Introduction: Home care organizations today are under pressure to do more with less as budgets tighten. Since staffing is by far the biggest expense in elder care (around 60% of total costs), optimizing how staff time is used can yield substantial savings. The key is to reduce inefficiencies without compromising quality of care. Smart scheduling and planning allow you to trim waste and make operations more efficient, so that every dollar (or krona) goes further. In fact, modern AI-driven scheduling can help achieve a triple-win: lower costs, better care quality, and improved working conditions for staff. Here are five concrete ways that better scheduling can help cut costs in home care.

1. Shorter travel times = lower transportation costs
   Travel between client visits is a necessary part of home care, but if not optimized, it can consume a lot of time and fuel. By improving route planning in your schedules, you can significantly reduce both the miles driven and the time spent in transit. In logistics terms, efficient routing “frees up work time and reduces costs” for the organization. For example, AI-based scheduling tools like CAIRE have achieved about a 20% reduction in total travel time for caregivers by optimizing routes and visit sequences. Fewer minutes behind the wheel translate directly into savings on fuel and vehicle wear-and-tear, and more minutes available for client care.

For care staff, smarter routing means less rushing around and more time caring for clients. For management, it means lower travel reimbursements and potentially being able to serve more clients per shift. Practical steps include grouping client visits by geography – ensure that caregivers’ schedules keep them in one area rather than crisscrossing town. Advanced scheduling software can do this clustering automatically and even adjust start times or break times to avoid traffic or peak congestion. Many municipalities have found huge efficiency gains here; for instance, one analysis showed that by reorganizing home care into local hubs, a Swedish municipality cut driving distance by 44%, dramatically reducing travel-related costs. Cutting down travel not only saves money, it also lessens environmental impact and caregiver fatigue – truly a win-win. 2. Less overtime through smarter staffing
Overtime pay can quickly drain a budget, and excessive overtime can also lead to staff burnout. Better scheduling aims to minimize overtime by aligning staffing levels with client needs at all times. A core principle is having the right number of caregivers at the right times. Often, overtime happens when schedules don’t fully match peak demand (e.g. not enough aides during the busy morning hours) or when unforeseen gaps occur. By analyzing patterns and using demand-driven scheduling, you can distribute work more evenly and avoid last-minute overtime. In fact, Sweden’s association of local authorities (SKR) notes that costs for overtime and temps can be reduced with properly planned, needs-based scheduling.

How to do this in practice? First, use historical data to identify when overtime is typically incurred – perhaps certain days of the week or times of day – and adjust the base schedule to boost staffing slightly at those times. It might involve staggering shifts or shortening some long shifts to create an extra short shift where needed. One real-world example comes from Marks kommun in Sweden. In 2024 they implemented new scheduling strategies in their home care, like shortening night shifts from 10 to 9 hours and ensuring more staff work every other weekend (instead of every third). As a result, the regular staff now cover more hours, drastically reducing the need for overtime or calling in extra help. This change not only saved money but also made workloads more equitable.

Modern scheduling software can also forecast and flag potential overtime before it happens. For instance, if a caregiver is heading towards exceeding their standard hours due to roster gaps, the system can alert managers to find a solution in advance. Another strategy is to build a small buffer of additional hours into some employees’ schedules (for example, scheduling someone at 90% time but paying 100%, so they have bandwidth to flex up without it being overtime). The bottom line: proactive planning beats reactive overtime scrambling every time. Less overtime means lower labor costs and a healthier staff, which can further reduce costs like sick leave. 3. Fewer instances of double-staffing
Double-staffing (sending two caregivers to the same client at once) is sometimes necessary for safety or logistical reasons, but it effectively doubles the labor cost for that visit. While certain care tasks (like heavy transfers or team-based care for a bedbound client) do require two people, often double-staffing happens simply due to scheduling inefficiencies or legacy routines. By scrutinizing where and when you really need two staff versus one, a smart scheduler can eliminate unnecessary double-ups.

One approach is to ensure each task in the care plan is clearly flagged if it requires two people; everything else should be assumed to be a one-person job unless explicitly needed. A scheduling system can then be instructed to only pair staff for those specific tasks. In cases where two staff are required, good planning can optimize how their time is used together. For example, if two caregivers are going to the same building for different clients who both need two-person assistance at some point, you can schedule their visits concurrently so they can help each other in turn, rather than doing two separate double-staffed trips. This way, you’re leveraging the presence of two staff in one location efficiently.

Reducing double-staffing also involves training and equipment – e.g., using assistive devices that allow single caregivers to safely perform transfers that used to need two. When such changes are made, the scheduling should be updated to reflect that fewer double visits are needed. Every avoided double-staff visit frees up one caregiver to attend to another client or task. Over time, the savings are significant: imagine cutting even 2 double-staff visits a day, that’s 2 extra visits one caregiver can do elsewhere (or simply time saved). Not to mention, it can empower staff by not overstaffing tasks that one person can manage, improving morale and autonomy.

In summary, scrutinize your schedule for any routine double-staff bookings and challenge whether they’re truly necessary. Smart scheduling systems make this easier by providing visibility and alternatives, ensuring you only invest two salaries where one won’t suffice. 4. Smarter use of substitutes (temps)
Last-minute call-outs and gaps due to vacations or sick leave often force home care providers to rely on temp staff or agency workers. These replacements can be costly – agencies charge premiums, and temporary workers may be less familiar (potentially needing more time to get things done). By improving schedule planning and flexibility, you can cut down on how often you need to pull in outside help or extra hours. SKR has observed that better scheduling can reduce the costs of both overtime and substitute staff by anticipating needs and planning accordingly.

One technique is to maintain a minimal “float pool” of part-time staff or cross-trained staff who can step in when needed. If your scheduling software can integrate with an availability list of part-timers who’ve previously worked with your clients, it can quickly find a match when someone calls in sick – often faster and cheaper than an external temp agency. But even better is preventing crises: use your system’s forecasting to see, for example, that five staff have requested vacation in July, and proactively arrange coverage by adjusting others’ schedules or hiring a summer temp in advance (usually at a lower cost than last-minute arrangements).

Marks kommun’s experience is instructive here. By changing how they scheduled regular staff (like having them cover more weekends), they dramatically reduced their reliance on hourly temps and overtime coverage. In fact, with the new schedules, the need for on-call temps dropped so much that the municipality saved 25 million SEK in one year, while still delivering better care. That’s roughly equivalent to 50 full-time positions worth of work reclaimed in a 700-employee workforce – an enormous efficiency gain.

The lesson is that a well-thought-out schedule provides stability. When your core team’s schedule already covers typical peak times and allows for some buffer, you’re not scrambling whenever someone is off. Of course, you can’t foresee every sickness, but you can create schedules that are resilient – e.g., avoid ever having a single point of failure (like only one person who can do a critical task on shift). Some advanced systems will even auto-suggest a fill-in from your internal pool when a gap arises, taking into account who’s worked with the client before or who is costing least in overtime. By using these tools, you’ll spend far less on emergency staffing, which directly improves the bottom line. 5. Lower staff turnover, lower training costs
Staff turnover is an often invisible cost driver. Hiring and training new caregivers is expensive and time-consuming, and frequent turnover can also degrade the quality of care (leading to other costs like client dissatisfaction or even hospitalizations). While many factors influence turnover, work schedules and work-life balance are among the biggest in care work. By providing fair, manageable schedules, you increase employee satisfaction and retention – which in turn saves money.

A good scheduling system contributes to lower turnover in several ways. First, it can help ensure equitable distribution of shifts (so that everyone shares the load of evenings, weekends, etc., as fairly as possible). Feeling overworked or that one’s schedule is unfair is a common reason employees quit. A transparent, algorithm-assisted schedule can remove bias and favoritism, leading to a greater sense of fairness. Second, advanced scheduling allows for more employee input and flexibility, such as accommodating preferred off-days or using algorithms to match schedules to individual preferences where possible. When caregivers feel their personal lives are respected, they are more likely to stay.

Crucially, smarter scheduling also ties back to continuity and workload – caregivers who consistently see the same clients often form bonds that make the job more rewarding (it’s more than just “tasks”, it’s relationships). This can improve job satisfaction and commitment to the organization. Additionally, eliminating stressors like constant last-minute changes, excessive overtime, or dreaded split shifts (working a morning and evening shift with a long break mid-day) can drastically improve morale. In Sweden, many providers are moving away from split shifts and offering more contiguous schedules, even if it means a slight cost increase in the short term, because they recognize it keeps staff healthier and happier. Those investments pay off by reducing sick leave and resignations.

Financially, the impact of lowering turnover is huge. Not only do you save on direct hiring costs, but experienced staff work more efficiently and require less supervision, and high retention often correlates with better care outcomes (avoiding costs like penalties or client attrition). One analysis (Vision, 2015) in Sweden estimated the cost of replacing a single full-time care worker at around 0.5–1 million SEK when all factors are included – recruitment, training, lost productivity, etc.. By this metric, if better schedules prevent even a few resignations a year, you’ve saved enough to justify a top-notch scheduling system. In summary, investing in your schedule is investing in your staff, and that pays off in loyalty and lower long-term costs.
Conclusion: Plan smart to save smart
The five strategies above – cutting travel time, reducing overtime, avoiding double-staffing, managing temp usage, and improving retention – all come down to one thing: smarter planning and scheduling. By leveraging data and modern scheduling technology, home care providers can unlock efficiency improvements that were previously hard to see. It’s not about making caregivers work harder; it’s about working smarter as an organization. When Marks kommun revamped their scheduling with a data-driven approach, they saved 25 million SEK in one year and even saw better care outcomes. That’s an impressive testament to the power of planning – those savings were achieved by reallocating existing resources more intelligently, equivalent to the work of 50 full-time staff that was re-optimized.

Crucially, these cost reductions did not come from cutting client services – they came from trimming waste and friction in the system. Every hour of staff time saved from driving or waiting is an hour that can be used to care for someone. Every dollar saved on overtime or agency fees is a dollar that can be reinvested in training or quality improvement. In an era where demand for home care is rising and budgets are tight, optimizing schedules is one of the most humane and high-impact ways to control costs. It lets you maintain or even improve the quality of care while keeping expenses in check.

In essence, efficient scheduling = cost savings + quality maintenance. Providers that harness AI and intelligent planning now will be better positioned to handle the growing eldercare needs without breaking the bank. It’s about creating a sustainable model where caregivers have balanced workloads, clients see consistent service, and managers have clear oversight of where resources are going.
